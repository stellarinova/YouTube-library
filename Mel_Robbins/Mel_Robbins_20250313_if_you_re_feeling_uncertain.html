If You’re Feeling Uncertain & Anxious, You Need to Hear This | Dr. Julie on The Mel Robbins Podcast

Channel: Mel Robbins
Published: March 13, 2025
Anxiety and uncertainty are not personal defects; they are part of being human. On The Mel Robbins Podcast, clinical psychologist Dr. Julie Smith offers a practical, unflinching guide to navigating fear, big feelings, and messy relationships-with tools you can use today.

You're Not Alone

One in four people will face a mental health challenge this year. Dr. Julie's rice metaphor makes it plain: even when you feel like the lone wild grain in a bowl of white, you're surrounded by others struggling too. Naming your struggle as human-not a failure-cuts shame and opens room for curiosity and help.

Build a Toolkit, Not a Myth

Therapy skills are life skills. Dr. Julie's book, Open When, is designed for the moments when you can't meditate your way out-short chapters, concrete actions, and a steady voice that says, Follow me. The aim is not perfection; it's direction.

Predator, Not Prey

After a sudden cancer diagnosis, Dr. Julie rewired her self-talk: This isn't hunting me-I'm hunting it. You can't delete fear, but you can decide your stance. Predators move toward a target; prey flinches at threats. In uncertainty, narrow your focus to the next right step and live as if you have a future.

Emotions Are Information

Feelings aren't flaws; they're signals. Treat waves of grief, anger, or panic like ocean swells: don't brace and topple; bend your knees and ride. When possible, anchor to a trusted person; when alone, let the feeling pass through and ask, What is this pointing to-rest, connection, protection, change?

Aim the Spotlight

Your attention is a spotlight on a crowded mental stage. You can't yank every anxious actor off, but you can choose what gets the light. In social anxiety, shift the beam outward: listen closely, notice details, ask small questions. Confidence grows from repeated reps of directing attention, not from erasing nerves.

Compare to Learn, Not to Lose

Comparison becomes poison when you turn friends into scoreboards. Make it useful: compare one narrow skill to someone a few steps ahead and copy what works. If resentment appears, treat it as a cue-are boundaries missing, or gratitude? Adjust there, not at the other person.

Be Your Own Coach

Living 24/7 with an inner bully keeps you small. Elite athletes hire coaches who are honest and on their side. Do the same in your head. A simple reset: I did the best I knew then; now I know more. Pull your focus from past (depression) and far future (anxiety) into the present, where action lives.

Choose by Values, Not Comfort

Comfort shrinks a life; values expand it. Do a quick audit: list domains (health, love, work, friends, parenting), write what matters in each, rate importance vs. how you're living now. Let the biggest gaps set your next actions. Values often ask for uncomfortable steps; that's how meaning is made.

Stop Letting Fear Shrink Your Life

Avoidance brings instant relief and long-term confinement. Dr. Julie's rainbow metaphor shows how life narrows one skipped layer at a time. Rebuild with an exposure ladder: start at the smallest doable challenge, repeat until it's boring, then step up. Dip a toe, don't dive-and keep going.

Real Relationships Are Forged, Not Found

You don't need to be fully healed to love or be loved. Strong bonds are shaped by weathering hard seasons together, not by perfect insight. You can't force a partner's style, but you can improve your own: regulate your emotions, repair with honesty (or humor), and remember that messy doesn't mean wrong.

When Anxiety Spikes

Drop judgment, add curiosity: Is this feeling warranted and proportionate-or am I vulnerable (tired, wired, isolated)? Meet the need: connection, rest, food, movement, clarity. Anxiety is an experience, not a verdict on your character.

Key Takeaways

  • Normalize struggle; remove shame to create space for action.
  • Adopt the predator stance: narrow your focus and move toward a target.
  • Treat emotions as data; let waves pass and listen for needs.
  • Direct your attention deliberately; confidence follows reps.
  • Make comparisons specific and constructive; use resentment as a boundary cue.
  • Coach yourself, don't bully yourself; act from values, not comfort.
  • Reverse avoidance with a gentle, repeated exposure ladder.
  • Healthy relationships are built through imperfect repairs, not perfect people.
