How to Design Your Life in 1 Hour

Channel: Mel Robbins
Published: 20260223
Two Stanford designers, Dave Evans and Bill Burnett, have spent two decades teaching a simple idea with radical consequences: there is no single right life to find—only many good lives to build. In a world that feels anxious, distracted, and short on meaning, they offer a one-hour starter kit: reframe the problem, sketch multiple futures, and prototype your way into the next chapter.

There’s No Right Life—Only Next Lives

Forget the myth of a single, perfect self awaiting discovery. Most of us harbor seven or eight viable lives inside us. If you only live one, you’re sampling roughly 14% of your full personhood. That’s not a crisis—it’s permission. Stop aiming for the final answer and start a new chapter. The design stance: you find your way by living it, not by predicting it.

From Meaning-of-Life to Meaning-in-Life

Designers practice problem finding before problem solving. The wrong question—What is my one true purpose?—keeps you stuck. The right one—How can I create more meaning here, now?—opens options. Meaning often hides in the flow world (the awakened brain): moments of deep absorption and aliveness found in running, cooking, painting, serving, or learning. Balance the transactional world (achieving brain) with regular visits to flow. It’s easier than you think.

The Odyssey Plan: Three Futures in 12 Minutes

Draft three five-year sketches: 1) If nothing changes and it goes well, where are you? 2) If Plan A disappears, what’s your Plan B? 3) The wild card: money no object, no one laughs—what then? The point isn’t to quit your job for the circus; it’s to quiet the inner critic and surface real alternatives. In thousands of workshops, peers almost always say the wild card is more doable than you think.

Prototype Your Next Chapter

Don’t bet the farm. Run low-stakes experiments to learn, not to win. Conduct narrative conversations (“What’s it like to be you?”), shadow someone, do a ride-along, try a weekend gig, publish a short piece before launching a podcast. This builds failure immunity: each small test yields data and confidence. Set the bar low—and clear it. Then iterate.

Age Isn’t a Deadline; It’s a Design Constraint

At 20, your brain’s executive function is still forming; your job is to give your 28-year-old self options. At 54, medical school might still leave you with a decade of practice. Systems are real, but so is agency. Choose where to push (policy, community, craft) and where to adapt. Trade FOMO for JOMO: every big yes comes bundled with many joyful nos.

Tiny Practices That Change Everything

- Post-it blueprint: Get curious. Talk to people. Try stuff. Tell your story—repeat. - Seventh-day savoring: five minutes weekly to relive one moment you felt deeply alive. - Eulogy now: write (or hear) the words you hope will be true; live toward them. - Focus question: craft a becoming question for this season (e.g., “How do I live from ‘get to’ not ‘got to’?”) and share it with a small circle. - Phone audit: reclaim 20 minutes from doomscrolling for reflection or a micro-prototype.

There’s no getting it right. There’s just getting it going.
You’re far bigger than your lifetime.
Your 20-year-old job is to give your 28-year-old self some interesting options.
Life is a series of incremental prototypes.
Get curious, talk to people, try stuff, tell your story.

Key Takeaways

  • Sketch three Odyssey plans to surface real options and quiet the inner critic.
  • Prototype with low-stakes tests—conversations, shadows, short sprints—before big bets.
  • Spend more time in the flow world to generate meaning now, not someday.
  • Write a eulogy and a focus question; share them with trusted people to live into them.
  • Replace FOMO with JOMO: commit to a path and celebrate the many lives you won’t live.
