Don’t Believe Everything You Think: How these 3 Cognitive Distortions make you miserable

Channel: Therapy in a Nutshell
Published: June 15, 2023
We tend to blame bosses, drivers, or awkward moments for our worst moods. But it's often our thoughts-not events-that light the fuse. Psychologist Albert Ellis mapped three rigid "musts" that silently wind us up. Learn to spot them, swap them, and feel steadier.

When Feelings Blame the World

Cognitive distortions are mental habits that twist reality and inflame emotion. We say, "That made me angry," when the real accelerant is how we think about it. CBT-cognitive behavioral therapy-starts with this idea: change the thought patterns, and emotions follow.

Ellis's Three Musts

1) I must do well and be approved by everyone-or I'm no good. Perfectionism, social anxiety, and people-pleasing grow here. 2) Others must treat me fairly, kindly, and prioritize my feelings-or they're bad. Cue resentment and conflict. 3) The world must give me what I want when I want it-or life is unbearable. Entitlement and chronic frustration thrive here.

Why Rigid Beliefs Hurt

"Musts" turn preferences into ultimatums. Reality rarely complies, so anxiety, depression, and anger bloom. Ellis jokingly called this pattern "musturbation": demanding the world be different instead of working with it.

Spot It: A Two-Minute Audit

Write down a recent situation that upset you. Scan for tells: catastrophizing when you're not perfect; dread that someone might dislike you; anger when plans change; bristling if praise isn't instant; irritation at lines, rules, or delays because they "shouldn't" exist. Notice which must is driving.

Swap It: The Five-Step Reset

1) Identify the belief: What "must" is underneath? 2) Challenge it: What evidence supports it? What contradicts it? Would you demand this of a friend? 3) Replace it: Trade absolutes for flexibles-"I prefer approval, but I can handle disagreement." 4) Practice behaviors that match the new belief: show up, share reasonably, stop ruminating. 5) Monitor and celebrate small wins: reinforce the new groove. Example: Post-book club anxiety. Core belief: "Everyone must approve of me." Challenge: Is universal approval possible? Likely that others fixated on your slip? Replace: "It's okay to be open; I can learn and be mindful next time." Practice: keep attending, limit replaying the scene. Track wins: easier conversations, less dread.

Road-Tested: Defusing Driving Rage

Anger behind the wheel often runs on Must #2: "Others must prioritize my needs." Challenge the story: Are all drivers terrible, or just imperfect humans? Replace: "My job is to arrive safely; others make mistakes." Control what you can: drive defensively, change lanes, or pull over and call for help if needed. Add a brief compassion script-"May they be safe; may I be calm"-to lower the temperature fast.

Make It Stick

Attention is fertilizer. Notice micro-victories-one calmer commute, one unruminated chat. That feedback loop boosts motivation and makes flexible thinking your new default.

Don't believe everything you think.
Identify, challenge, replace, practice, reward-then repeat.

Key Takeaways

  • Emotions are often amplified by rigid thoughts, not events.
  • Ellis's three musts-self, others, world-create predictable misery.
  • Swap absolutes for preferences to reduce anxiety, anger, and shame.
  • Use the five-step reset to challenge and replace unhelpful beliefs.
  • Reinforce small wins; consistency rewires the habit.
