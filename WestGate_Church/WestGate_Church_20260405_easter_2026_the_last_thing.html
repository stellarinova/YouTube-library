On Easter, a pastor makes a countercultural claim: regret is not the end of the story. From a misfired text that created a chosen family to an ancient pattern hidden in the Bible’s first pages, he argues Easter is less a redo than a radical rewrite—a new beginning breaking into ordinary life.

A Wrong Number, a Right Turn

In 2016, a stray text invited teenager Jamal Hinton to Thanksgiving. When he confessed he wasn’t the intended grandson and asked if he could still get a plate, the sender—Wanda Dench—said yes. Ten Thanksgivings later, they’re family. It’s the kind of reversal we secretly wish for: a mistake that becomes a mercy.

The Weight of Regret

Psychologists call regret a "cognitive emotion" because it seizes both mind and heart. We replay what happened while imagining what should have been, and the loop tightens. Who wouldn’t want to undo the damage, repair the missed chances, and see great wrongs made right? The ache is universal.

Easter Isn’t a Renewal Notice

We hear "renewal" and think DMV paperwork—same thing, new sticker. The Christian claim is far bolder: not an upgrade but a new creation. As the apostle Paul puts it, though the outer life frays, the inner life can be "renewed day by day"—a daily remaking that begins now, not someday.

Creation, Recreated

Page one of the Bible opens with light, the first day, Sabbath, and a garden. The resurrection retells the scene: after the Sabbath, at dawn on the first day, lightning-like messengers—and a figure mistaken for a gardener. The pattern isn’t accidental. Easter reads like Genesis rewritten, signaling a world set right from the ground up.

Open the Door

A young woman named Grace found faith not by force but by invitation. A 19th-century painting of Jesus knocking at a door with no exterior handle clicked: love doesn’t kick its way in. You open from the inside. For her, that simple act reframed the past and made room for a different future.

Freedom, Not Numbness

Modern life tempts us toward anesthesia and amnesia—numbing the pain or erasing it. Easter’s offer is neither. It doesn’t pretend the past didn’t happen; it breaks its grip. The point is not to forget but to be freed, to become more alive than the wounds that named us.

From the Small to the Shattering

There are everyday stings—snapping at a child and wishing for a do-over—and life-shaping losses, like estrangement from a parent. Even here the story can bend. A late discovery of a father’s faith became the seed of a future reconciliation, the kind that time alone could never broker.

Stop Looking for Life in Dead Places

The Easter narratives insist the grave is empty and the burden carried away. If that’s true, we can stop combing the wreckage of yesterday for breath. The invitation is stubbornly practical: quit returning to the scene of the hurt, and let the living interrupt what feels finished.

The Last Word

Frederick Buechner wrote that the worst thing is not the last thing because the last thing is the best thing. Easter stakes everything on that line. The promise isn’t neatness; it’s newness—the surprising turn where the story refuses to end where we thought it had to.

Key Takeaways

  • Regret binds both thought and feeling, trapping us in endless what-ifs.
  • Easter frames renewal as new creation, not a cosmetic upgrade.
  • The resurrection mirrors Genesis—first day, light, Sabbath, garden—signaling creation remade.
  • Opening to grace is an act of agency: the door has no handle on the outside.
  • Jesus offers freedom over numbness, presence over erasure.
  • Even ordinary failures and old wounds can be rewritten into living hope.
