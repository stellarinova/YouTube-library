When It Feels Like Anxiety But It's Actually ANGER - with Dr. Sarah Michaud

Therapy in a Nutshell Deep Dive •August 14, 2025
We’re quick to label the racing heart, sleepless nights, and looping thoughts as anxiety. But often, what’s humming underneath is anger—unfelt, unnamed, and cleverly displaced. Psychologist and addiction counselor Dr. Sarah Michaud argues that learning to recognize and metabolize anger can quiet a lifelong fretfulness—and sometimes even stop panic in its tracks.

The Misdiagnosed Emotion

Anger and anxiety often travel together, but we tend to see only one. Many of us were trained—especially women—to avoid anger at all costs. So it goes underground, reappearing as worry, irritability, insomnia, and a brittle need to keep the peace.

An AA Inventory That Changed Everything

Early in sobriety, Michaud swore she wasn’t angry. After a rigorous resentment inventory, she counted 486 resentments—and watched her anxiety drop as she faced them. The lesson: you don’t have to feel red‑hot rage to be run by anger; quiet resentments do the job.

Hidden Anger, Visible Symptoms

Clues that anger is underground: chronic anxiety or depression, over-accommodation, control or criticism, sarcasm, busyness, lateness, addictions, body pain, fatigue, insomnia. If your reaction is bigger than the moment, Michaud says, it’s likely old.

From Powerlessness to Rage: A Simple Pyramid

Michaud maps anger like this: at the base, powerlessness; above it, fear; then sadness and grief (with anxiety woven in); then irritation and anger; at the top, rage. When we feel powerless and scared, anger becomes armor. But armor can suffocate.

When the Body Says No

Anger shows up in jaws, backs, guts, sleep—anywhere tension lives. Gabor Maté links chronic people-pleasing to illness; John Sarno traced many back pains to chronic, repressed anger. Somatic work often reveals what talk can’t: hands that want to push, a throat that wants to say no.

Resentment Work, Step by Step

Start by naming it: who am I angry at, and how does it affect my self-esteem, security, relationships, ambitions? Then do the turnaround: What did I want? Were my expectations realistic? What story did I tell about myself (I’m unlovable), and what’s the truer story? What ineffective behaviors do I use—avoidance, manipulation, blame—and what fear drives them (rejection, abandonment)?

Processing Anger Without Burning Bridges

Don’t blast it; metabolize it. Write an anger letter (do not send), then write the sadness and the healthy wish beneath it. Try a 10-minute freewrite: What am I angry, sad, or scared about? Move it through your body—exercise, a hard push against a wall, a scream in the car. Pause before you act. Practice assertiveness: clear no’s, clean requests, and acceptance of what you can’t control.

Everyday Frictions, Old Wounds

Dismissiveness from a boss or in-law can ignite decades-old powerlessness. Call it what it is—an activation—and choose a different move: define your boundary, extend compassion, and respond after you’ve cooled. With kids, welcome the voice, shape the delivery: disagreement is allowed; disrespect isn’t.

Key Takeaways

  • Anxiety often masks anger—especially in people conditioned to be “nice.”
  • Hidden anger leaks as worry, perfectionism, people-pleasing, and physical pain.
  • Use a resentment inventory to name the anger and see how it harms your life.
  • Turnarounds reveal unrealistic expectations, false self-stories, and the fears driving them.
  • Somatic cues—tight jaws, hot hands, a pushing impulse—often point to boundary violations.
  • Process anger privately: write it out, move it, cry; don’t dump it on others.
  • Assertiveness is skillful anger: clear limits, clean requests, no control over others.
  • Beware fancied resentments—stories you invent without facts.
  • The goal isn’t catharsis; it’s clarity, compassion, and consistent boundaries.
  • As you befriend anger, anxiety and depression usually ease.
