This One Thing Is Giving You Anxiety | Kevin Thompson

Channel: Focus on the Family
Published: April 02, 2026
If the first thing you touch each morning is your phone, your day may already be set to anxious. Pastor and author Kevin Thompson argues for a simple but radical swap: start with what's eternally true before the feed that profits from your fear. The result isn't denial of reality-it's a steadier way to face it.

The Morning We Choose

Doomscrolling-mindlessly consuming bad news-turns sunrise into a stress test. Thompson's counterintuitive move: begin with hope. Read a short passage of Scripture or a timeless truth before headlines. Think of it as calibrating your internal weather before stepping into the storm.

Why Outrage Wins

Platforms spend billions to capture attention. The cheapest fuel is anger: we click more when we're mad. That's why good news rarely trends. Hope-filled people scroll less, which is bad for ad revenue-but good for your soul.

Pick Your Story First

Thompson describes two stories shaping every day: the higher story (the Christian claim that God is present, loving, and at work) and the lower story (the churn of news and tasks). Both are real. The one you enter first frames how you interpret the other.

Proof in the Practice

A Fellowship of Christian Athletes study found no measurable change for students who engaged Scripture 2-3 days a week. At four days, scores on anxiety, discouragement, and conduct shifted dramatically. Not perfection-rhythm. A steady, modest practice rewires outlook.

Two Minutes That Save a Marriage

A couple tried one fix: at 2:00 p.m., he set a two-minute alarm to think about his wife's day and how to arrive helpfully; she did the same at nap time for him. Six weeks later: "Nothing has changed, but everything has changed." Attention created empathy; empathy changed evenings.

When Hope Found a Prison Cell

A former student, relapsing and incarcerated, decided to become an atheist, returned his Bible, and turned on a prison radio. The first voice he heard: Thompson's interview on owning your choices and surrendering the rest to God. His letter back: "I was an atheist for 30 minutes." The point isn't spectacle-it's the stubborn way hope can meet us where we are.

Three Questions to Reframe the Day

1) What's the hope about God? Note who God is-character before circumstance. 2) How is that hope for me today? Apply it to your exact moment-hospital room, carpool line, board meeting. 3) How can I share this hope? End not with yourself but with someone you can encourage-start at home, then outward to colleagues and neighbors.

Hope in Hard Places

Mocking a broken culture is easy; serving it is harder. Thompson's California lesson: don't flee simply out of cynicism. Weep, then work. The call isn't escape but participation-bringing a different spirit into public life, one marked by patience, courage, and practical help.

Start Tomorrow Differently

Before news, read a brief passage and mark every reference to God; ask the three questions; set a two-minute empathy alarm; limit reactive feeds until midday. Small, consistent choices replace anxiety's reflex with hope's reflex.

Key Takeaways

  • Your first input of the day frames your outlook.
  • Algorithms reward outrage; choose practices that cultivate hope.
  • Four days a week of Scripture engagement can materially shift anxiety and behavior.
  • Brief daily attention builds empathy and transforms relationships.
  • End your preparation by asking how to share hope-start at home, then move outward.
